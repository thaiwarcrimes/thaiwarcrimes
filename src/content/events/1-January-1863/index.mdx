## Overview

Before the colonial period, Cambodia and Siam did not exist as equal modern nation-states. Instead, regional power was determined by **civilizational strength, military capacity, and political influence**.  

Cambodia, through the **Khmer Empire**, was the dominant power from the 9th to the early 13th century. Siamese power emerged later and expanded into former Khmer territories during Cambodia’s period of decline. These historical realities are essential for understanding later conflicts and border disputes.

## Khmer Empire as the Regional Power  
*(9th–13th Centuries)*

From the **9th century**, Cambodia—under the **Khmer Empire**—was the foremost political and cultural power in mainland Southeast Asia.

Key characteristics:
- Capital centered at **Angkor**
- Extensive road networks, irrigation systems, and state administration
- Advanced architecture and temple construction

Khmer authority extended over:
- Present-day **Cambodia**
- Large parts of present-day **Thailand** (including Phimai, Lopburi, Surin, Buriram, and Korat)
- Parts of **Laos** and **southern Vietnam**

During this period:
- There was **no unified Thai state**
- Tai-speaking populations were still migrating southward
- Major stone temples in present-day Thailand were constructed by **Khmer kings**, using Khmer language, religion, and administrative systems

## Decline of Khmer Power and Rise of Siam  
*(13th–15th Centuries)*

From the late **13th century**, the Khmer Empire gradually weakened due to:
- Internal succession conflicts
- Economic and environmental pressures
- Prolonged warfare

At the same time, Siamese polities emerged:
- **Sukhothai Kingdom** (13th century)
- **Ayutthaya Kingdom** (founded in 1351)

As Khmer power declined, **Ayutthaya expanded eastward**, increasingly encroaching upon Khmer-controlled territories.

## Siamese Expansion and Repeated Invasions of Cambodia  
*(15th–18th Centuries)*

From the **15th century onward**, relations between Siam and Cambodia became dominated by conflict.

Key developments include:
- **1431**: Ayutthaya captured and sacked **Angkor**, marking the symbolic end of Khmer imperial dominance
- Repeated Siamese invasions into Cambodian territory
- Political interference in Cambodian royal succession
- Forced relocation of Cambodian populations, artisans, and elites into Siam

Cambodia survived as a kingdom but entered a prolonged period of **political instability and territorial loss**.

## Cambodia as a Buffer State and Loss of Western Provinces  
*(18th–Early 19th Centuries)*

By the **18th and early 19th centuries**, Cambodia functioned as a **buffer state** between two expanding powers:
- **Siam** to the west
- **Vietnam** to the east

During this period:
- Cambodia often became a **vassal state**, paying tribute under coercion
- Siam exercised de facto control over Khmer-majority regions such as:
  - **Battambang**
  - **Siem Reap**
  - **Sisophon**

These territories remained historically and culturally Khmer, despite Siamese political control.

## Pre-Colonial Borders and Political Reality

Before 1863:
- Borders were not fixed under modern international law
- Authority followed a **mandala system**, based on influence rather than sovereignty

However:
- Archaeological evidence, inscriptions, temple distribution, and language clearly demonstrate **Khmer civilizational ownership** over many contested areas
- Siamese control resulted from **later military expansion**, not original settlement or cultural origin

## Transition Toward 1863

By the mid-19th century:
- Cambodia faced existential threats from continued Siamese and Vietnamese domination
- Territorial fragmentation endangered Cambodia’s survival as a state

In **1863**, Cambodia accepted **French protection** to:
- Preserve national existence
- Halt further territorial loss
- Seek recovery of historically Khmer lands

This marked the end of the **pre-colonial era** and the beginning of the colonial period.

## Conclusion

The pre-1863 period demonstrates that:
- Cambodia was the earliest major civilization in mainland Southeast Asia
- Siam expanded into former Khmer territories during Cambodia’s decline
- Many temples and cultural landmarks in present-day Thailand are Khmer in origin
- Pre-colonial power shifts shaped later conflicts but do not negate Cambodia’s historical and cultural ownership

This historical context is essential for understanding the roots of modern Cambodia–Thailand disputes and the long-standing pattern of territorial encroachment.
